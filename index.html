Through the Ages

History of Tulunadu

From the ancient Alupa kingdom to the vibrant present — a story woven across two thousand years.

2nd Century CE

Alupa Dynasty

The Alupas, one of the oldest ruling families of coastal Karnataka, establish dominion over the Tulunadu region, patronising Jainism and Hindu traditions simultaneously.

8th – 10th Century

Rise of Yakshagana

The seeds of Yakshagana — the grand theatrical tradition of Tulunadu — are sown during this period, blending devotion, drama, dance, and music into an art form unlike any other.

14th – 16th Century

Vijayanagara Influence

Under the Vijayanagara Empire, Tulunadu flourishes as a cultural and economic hub. Major temple complexes are expanded, and the region becomes renowned for its maritime trade.

15th Century

Tulu Script & Literature

The ancient Tigalari (Tulu) script, closely related to Grantha, is used to write sacred texts and poetic works, preserving the oral traditions of Tulu literature in written form.

17th – 18th Century

Keladi Nayaka & Kodagu Rule

The Keladi Nayakas take control of Tulunadu, followed by Hyder Ali and Tipu Sultan. Despite political changes, the cultural traditions of Bhuta Kola and Yakshagana continue unbroken.

Present Day

Living Heritage

Tulunadu's traditions remain vibrantly alive. Bhuta Kola ceremonies are performed in hundreds of villages, Yakshagana troupes travel across the world, and Tulu cinema brings its culture to new generations.

Living Traditions

Culture & Heritage

Tulunadu is not merely a region — it is a living museum of traditions that have shaped coastal Karnataka for centuries.

🔥

Bhuta Kola

A ritualistic spirit worship unique to Tulunadu, where performers channel ancestral spirits (Bhutas) through elaborate costumes, dance, and oral epics. A UNESCO-recognised tradition of immeasurable cultural depth.

🎭

Yakshagana

A spectacular theatrical form combining dance, music, dialogue, and elaborate costumes. Performed all night under the open sky, Yakshagana brings epics like the Mahabharata and Ramayana to life with unmatched energy.

🌊

Kambala

The ancient buffalo race of Tulunadu — a harvest festival tradition where buffaloes and their handlers sprint through flooded paddy fields, celebrating the bond between people, animals, and the land.

🍛

Tulu Cuisine

From Kori Rotti (chicken curry with crispy rice wafers) to Patrode (colocasia leaf rolls) and Neer Dosa, Tulu cuisine is a celebration of coastal flavours — coconut-rich, aromatic, and deeply satisfying.

✍️

Tulu Language

One of the ancient Dravidian languages with its own literary tradition, Tulu is spoken by nearly 2 million people. Rich in proverbs, folk songs (Paddanas), and oral epics, it carries the soul of the region.

🤼

Aati Kalenja

A monsoon folk tradition where performers dressed as Kalenja visit households, singing blessings and warding off evil. An intimate, community-level expression of Tulu folk belief and seasonal celebration.

Sacred Places

Sacred Temples

Tulunadu's temples are not merely places of worship — they are living institutions that anchor communities across centuries.

Shri Dharmasthala Temple

Dharmasthala

A unique temple managed by a Jain family for a Hindu deity, Dharmasthala embodies Tulunadu's centuries-old tradition of interfaith harmony and charitable service.

Shri Krishna Matha

Udupi

Founded by philosopher-saint Madhvacharya in the 13th century, the Udupi Krishna temple is one of India's most revered Vaishnava shrines and birthplace of Udupi cuisine.

Kukke Subramanya Temple

Subramanya

Nestled at the foot of the Western Ghats, this ancient temple dedicated to Lord Subramanya draws pilgrims for the Sarpa Samskara rituals, believed to cure serpent-related afflictions.

Kateel Durgaparameshwari Temple

Kateel, near Mangalore

Set on an island in the Nandini river, this goddess temple is one of Tulunadu's most beloved, drawing devotees in enormous numbers for its annual Brahmakalashotsava festival.

Moodabidri Jain Temples

Moodabidri

Known as the "Jain Kashi of Karnataka," Moodabidri has 18 Jain basadis (temples), with the 1000-pillar Thousand Pillars Basadi being a masterpiece of medieval Jain architecture.

Kollur Mookambika Temple

Kollur

High in the Kodachadri hills, the Mookambika temple is one of the six sacred abodes of Adi Shakti, drawing pilgrims on a journey through some of Karnataka's most spectacular natural scenery.
